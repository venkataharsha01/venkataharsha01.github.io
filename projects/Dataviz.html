Key Findings

What the data reveals

Across 7 official Pacific Data Hub datasets spanning 55+ years, four critical patterns emerge that define the region's climate-economic vulnerability.

01
The Pacific has warmed by +0.72°C relative to the 1991–2020 climatological baseline in 2024, with acceleration visible after 2015 — a trend consistent with global climate projections.
02
Fiji alone accounts for 64% of total disaster economic loss across all Pacific nations — driven largely by Cyclone Winston in 2016.
03
Average crop yield has declined -2.36% from early years to recent decades, coinciding with a regional rainfall deficit of -0.83 mm below historical averages.

Methodology

How this was built

All data sourced from the Pacific Data Hub's official .Stat Explorer. Seven datasets processed and combined to produce this analysis.

📊
Data Sources
7 official Pacific Data Hub datasets covering GHG emissions, temperature anomalies, sea level, rainfall, crop yield, disaster loss, persons affected, and renewable energy.
🔧
Tools Used
Power BI for interactive dashboard. Python for data cleaning and vulnerability index calculation. HTML/CSS/ECharts for this narrative website.
📐
Vulnerability Index
Min-max normalized composite of 4 indicators: GHG (20%), Disaster Loss (35%), Persons Affected (25%), Crop Yield vulnerability (20%).
🌏
Coverage
22 Pacific Island Countries and Territories. Year ranges vary by dataset: 1850–2025 (temperature), 1961–2024 (crop yield), 2005–2023 (disaster data).
Research Disclaimer
This analysis is based on official datasets from the Pacific Data Hub and presents historical patterns and statistical associations across Pacific Island nations. The custom Climate-Economic Vulnerability Index was developed specifically for this project using min–max normalization and analyst-defined indicator weights. It is intended as an analytical tool for comparison and visualization rather than an official or standardized measure of climate vulnerability. Observed relationships should not be interpreted as evidence of direct causation, as socioeconomic outcomes are influenced by multiple environmental, economic, and policy factors.
